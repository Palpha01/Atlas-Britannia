General information

Wales is one of the four constituent countries of the United Kingdom, located west of England and east of the Irish Sea and Ireland.

It is known for its rugged landscape and Celtic culture, which includes the Welsh language, though not all Welsh people speak it.

The population of Wales is around three million, with most people living in the southern and southeastern parts of the country. Cardiff, the capital and largest city, has a population of around 363,000.

Demographics

Wales has a population of 3,107,500 as of the 2021 census. The majority of the population is white, with 94% of the population identifying as White British.

The population is predominantly concentrated in South Wales, including the cities of Cardiff, Swansea, and Newport, as well as the nearby valleys and the north-east around Wrexham and Flintshire.

The total fertility rate in Wales was 1.48 in 2021, which is below the replacement rate.

Eatery

Cawl is a traditional Welsh dish that is a hearty stew made of meat and vegetables, often varying by family and region in Wales. It is typically made with lamb or beef, leeks, potatoes, swedes, carrots, and other seasonal vegetables. Cawl is recognized as a national dish of Wales and has recipes dating back to the fourteenth century. It is often better the day after preparing when all the flavors have developed.
Welsh cakes are a traditional sweet bread in Wales. They have been popular since the late 19th century and are made from flour, butter or lard, currants, eggs, milk, and spices such as cinnamon and nutmeg. Welsh cakes are a cross between a cookie, scone, and pancake but are unique in taste and texture. They are a beloved part of Welsh culinary heritage and are enjoyed as an everyday treat or on special occasions.

Traditions

Arthurian myth is a rich tapestry of stories and romances centered around the legendary King Arthur and his court. The character of King Arthur developed through Welsh mythology, appearing as a great warrior defending Britain from human and supernatural enemies or as a magical figure of folklore.
An Eisteddfod is a Welsh competitive festival of the arts, particularly in poetry and singing, and it has a long history dating back to the 12th century. The term "Eisteddfod" means "sitting-together" in Welsh, referring to the assembly of bards and minstrels who would sit together for poetry and music competitions.

Landmarks

The Caernarfon castle was built on the site of an earlier motte-and-bailey castle, which was constructed in the late 11th century. The current stone structure was begun in 1283 and was still incomplete by about 1330 when major work ended. The castle's walls are reminiscent of the Walls of Constantinople, and there was a deliberate link with Caernarfon's Roman past—nearby is the Roman fort of Segontium.
Snowdonia, also known as Eryri, is a mountainous region and national park in North Wales. It is renowned for its stunning landscapes, diverse wildlife, and rich cultural heritage. The park covers an area of 823 square miles (2,130 km²) and includes all 15 mountains in Wales that are over 3,000 feet high, with Snowdon (Yr Wyddfa) being the highest at 1,085 meters (3,560 ft) tall.

Fun Fact: The longest place name in Europe

The longest place name in Europe from Wales is a village named 'Llanfairpwllgwyngyllgogerychwyrndrobwllllantysiliogogogoch',

which translates into "St. Mary's Church in the hollow of the white hazel near a rapid whirlpool and the Church of St. Tysilio near the red cave."
