General information

The Crown Dependencies are three self-governing possessions of the British Crown: the Bailiwick of Jersey, the Bailiwick of Guernsey, and the Isle of Man. They are not part of the United Kingdom but are territories for which the UK is responsible. Each dependency has its own legislative assemblies, administrative, fiscal, and legal systems.

The Bailiwick of Guernsey includes the islands of Guernsey, Alderney, Sark, and Herm, with a total area of 78 km and a population of 67,334.

The Bailiwick of Jersey comprises the island of Jersey, with an area of 118.2 km and a population of 107,800.

The Isle of Man, located in the Irish Sea, has an area of 572 km and a population of 83,314.

Demographics

The Isle of Man, with a population of around 88,790 as of early 2025, has a population density of 148 people per square kilometer (382 people per square mile). The majority of the population lives in urban areas, with Douglas being the capital and largest town, having a population of about 27,000. The median age of the population is 46.1 years. English is the primary language spoken, while Manx, the native language of the island, was once considered extinct as the last native speaker passed away in 1974. The population growth rate is 0.84%, with a net migration rate of 7.11 migrants per 1,000 population.

The Bailiwick of Guernsey has a total population of 64,445 as of today. The population is evenly distributed between males and females, with 31,837 males (49.401%) and 32,608 females (50.599%). The population growth rate is 0.265%. Today, there are no births or deaths reported. The natural change in population for the year 2025 is an increase of 54 people (0.084%).

The Bailiwick of Jersey is a British Crown dependency with a population of 103,267 as of 2021. The capital city is Saint Helier. The population growth from 2011 to 2021 was 5,400, with 3,330 due to migration and 2,100 due to natural growth. Approximately 39% of the population identifies as non-religious. The island's economy is robust, contributing to a high standard of living, with a gross value added (GVA) of £3.6 billion in 2011, translating to £37,000 per head.

Eatery

Manx Queenies, are medium-sized queen scallops sustainably caught in the waters around the Isle of Man. They are known for their sweet flavor and can be found in various colors, including yellow, orange, brown, red, or purple. Isle of Man Queenies have been awarded the European Union Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) stamp and are considered a signature dish of the Isle of Man.
The Jersey Bean Crock, also known as "les pais au fou," is a traditional dish from the island of Jersey. It is a hearty pork and bean stew that has been a staple in Jersey households for generations. The Bean Crock represents the resilience and resourcefulness of Jersey's people and is a symbol of the island's culinary heritage.
Guernsey Gâche is a traditional sweet bread from Guernsey, a Channel Island. It is made with raisins, sultanas, cherries, and mixed peel. In the local Guernésiais language, "gâche" means cake, but it is more like a fruit bread than a traditional cake. Guernsey Gâche is often served with Guernsey butter and a pot of tea, making it a popular choice for a sweet treat or dessert.

Traditions

Tynwald Day is the National Day of the Isle of Man, usually observed on July 5th. The event is significant as it dates back to the 10th century and is known as the 'Midsummer Court.' It is attended by members of the two branches of Tynwald: the House of Keys and the Legislative Council.
Novelist Victor Hugo lived in Jersey for a period during his exile from France. He moved there in 1852 and stayed until 1855. In Jersey, Victor Hugo Wines is located at Tregear House, St. Saviour, and offers a range of products including wine, spirits, beer, and cider.
Guernsey has a rich tradition of folk dancing, influenced by both British and French cultures. Traditional folk dancing can be enjoyed at St Peter Port fairs and the Valestock Music Festival.

Landmarks

The Isle of Man TT (Tourist Trophy) is an annual motorcycle racing event held on the entire Isle of Man, running from May 26 to June 7, 2025. It consists of one week of practice and qualifying sessions followed by one week of racing on the 37.73-mile Snaefell Mountain Course, known for its high speeds and dangerous conditions.
Constructed in 1874, the La Corbière Lighthouse is the first lighthouse in the British Isles built of reinforced concrete. The lighthouse stands 19 meters (62 feet) tall and is visible up to 18 nautical miles away. It can be accessed via a causeway at low tide, but visitors must be cautious as the tide can rise quickly.
The islands of Guernsey and Alderney, part of the Balliwick, have a long history of fortifications. Castle Cornet is an 800-year-old castle located at the mouth of St Peter Port harbour in Guernsey. It has served as a defensive fortress and has been involved in numerous historical events, including the Hundred Years' War, Napoleonic Wars, and the German Occupation of Guernsey during World War II.

Fun Fact: Mini-nations within an Empire

These tax-havens are often referred to as "miniature states with wide powers of self-government," as noted in the 1973 Kilbrandon Report.

Each island has its own legislative assembly, with the power to legislate on many local matters with the assent of the Crown.
